Festival of the Mind

Identity Graphic design Delivery Project management

Launched in 2012 by the University of Sheffield, Festival of the Mind was created to bring research to life through creative, public-facing collaborations between academics and the cultural sector across South Yorkshire. Following its early success, the festival became a biennial event. From 2012 to 2024, I led the development of the festival’s visual identity, evolving it across each edition.

I worked closely with a team of 3D designers to create a brand that was both distinctive and flexible, ensuring it could adapt to the changing themes and formats of each festival.

To support the scale and diversity of the event, a cohesive and highly functional design system was essential. This needed to operate both as a recognisable brand and an effective wayfinding tool across the city.

I developed clear and accessible visual hierarchies to guide audiences from street-level communications to the digital programme. The identity was designed to engage a broad demographic, including families, while maintaining the credibility and academic standing of the University. Alongside this, I oversaw the production of large-scale print and advertising to ensure consistency across all touchpoints.

Applying a user-centred approach, I refined the festival’s visual language to improve clarity and immediate comprehension. Large-format advertising was simplified to prioritise key information, enabling audiences to quickly understand and engage with the programme.

I designed a wide range of materials, from 48-sheet billboards to printed programmes and digital assets, ensuring consistency throughout. Working closely with the University’s Cultural Engagement team, I managed the full print and production process to maintain quality and alignment with the overall design intent.

This resulted in a vibrant, city-wide presence that helped make the University’s research more accessible and engaging to the public. Through an iterative, feedback-led approach, the festival saw increased levels of public engagement and exhibition attendance across successive editions.
