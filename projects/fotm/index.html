Festival of the Mind

Identity Graphic design Delivery Project management

Overview

Launched in 2012 by the University of Sheffield, Festival of the Mind was created to bring research to life in creative, engaging ways by fostering collaboration between academics and the cultural sector in South Yorkshire. Due to its success, the festival became a biennial event, continuing to inspire and innovate today.

At Human Studio, I served as the lead designer for the festival’s visual identity from 2012 to 2024. My role involved working closely with a talented team of 3D designers to develop a brand that was adaptable and engaging for each festival edition, ensuring the visuals connected with the theme and spirit of the year.

What was needed

We needed to bridge the gap between a person seeing a billboard on a busy street and that same person actually attending an event or visiting the website. To do this the project required a cohesive design system that functioned as both a brand and a wayfinding tool:

  • Clear call-to-action: A streamlined path from physical street-level displays to the digital programme.
  • User-centred visuals: An identity that appealed to diverse demographics, specifically families, without losing the prestige of the University.
  • Production Management: End-to-end oversight of large-scale advertising and print to ensure the user experience remained consistent across every platform.

My approach

I applied a user-centred design lens to the festival’s history:

  • I refined the branding to improve clarity and ease of comprehension. By simplifying large-scale adverts, I made sure key information was instantly readable, allowing passersby to quickly understand the essentials at a glance.
  • I designed everything from 48-sheet billboards to handheld programmes, ensuring that the visual cues remained consistent as the user moved from the street into the exhibition spaces.
  • Working closely with the Cultural Engagement team at the university, I managed the full print and production cycle ensuring the festival’s physical presence matched the design intent and provided a seamless experience for the visitor.

The outcome

The result was a vibrant, city-wide takeover that successfully humanised the University’s research. By using an iterative, feedback-led approach, the university saw a measurable increase in public engagement and exhibition attendance compared to previous years.
